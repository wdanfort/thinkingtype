ThinkingType: how typography influences AI judgments and decisions

Does a VLM “read” a Comic Sans resume as unprofessional?

Will Danforth · Views are my own and not my employer’s · v1, last updated July 21st, 2026.

Short answer: Sort-of. Comic Neue (open source Comic Sans) at most flips “is this formal?” by up to +83pp versus plain Times, but I didn't find similar impact on "unprofessional-ness" . The far bigger influence is whether the model reads words as text or an image.

I built ThinkingType as a lightweight benchmark to measure how typography influences qualitative assessments (e.g. trustworthy, professional) and downstream decisions (“flag this policy violation,” “approve this resume”). My main findings:

  1. Presentation modality alone changes answers: showing a model an image of the same words (plain Times) instead of raw text flips ~10% of yes/no judgments.
  2. This effect differs significantly across Claude and GPT and changed with each new generation.
  3. Fable 5’s refusal rate varied about 20× by font; Monospace and OpenDyslexic refused at 6 to 7 times the rate of the calmest fonts, and no other model refused at all.
  4. On top of these effects, fonts do indeed move decisions:
    1. Comic Neue and OpenDyslexic are the likeliest to flip a judgment (up to 19% and 21% respectively); Comic Neue alone pushes GPT-5.5’s “is this formal?” answers by +83pp, while its effect on professionalism judgments is under +2pp.
    2. Times regular is the most stable font in every model I tested, with similar judgements as the corresponding LLM running on plain text.

I suspect the direction of these effects generalizes because the pattern shows up in every provider I tested. I just used synthetic examples, see limitations, so that's something I'm hoping to strengthen next time. Given that both the VLM vs. LLM and typography impacts shift across providers and models, I’m planning to track and publish new results against future major model releases.

I haven’t found prior work focused on downstream decisions and qualitative assessments. The recent work I’ve found on AI × typography focuses more on VLM accuracy, cybersecurity, and things like DecoyFont. The closest work I’ve seen is Reading, Not Thinking on how rendering text as pixels dulls model reasoning, and Visual Persuasion on how image styling sways VLM choices, but neither looks at fonts driving judgments or decisions.

All of the code, methodology, and data are publicly available on GitHub. I built the eval harness with Claude Code, which also helped with the writing. I want feedback on methodology and gaps, especially from anyone running VLMs on real documents in production, so please reach out.

Quick Product Implications

  1. Vision-text consistency. This property is not stable across provider, model, or generation. Typography should be included in internal product evals to identify drift.
  2. Equity. Fonts like OpenDyslexic fared much worse than traditional ones, so extra caution should be taken when building out evals to ensure fairness and equity.
  3. Influence Some fonts perform better than others. This is a limited set, but my hypothesis is that there are fonts that uniquely influence VLMs towards more positive outcomes such as viewing an author more favorably.
  4. Mitigations. I found prompt rubrics for decisions largely eliminated divergence between text and image presentations.
  5. Fable 5. Refusals clustered around Monospace and OpenDyslexic at around 3%. These rates are common enough that they would appear frequently in production, which might have further accessibility implications. GPT 5.6 Sol did not have a single refusal for the entire corpus.

Key Limitations

  1. All benchmark artifacts, including sentence examples, are synthetic, and so may not mirror real world results. I tried to generate them to be on the borderline for decisions and criteria. I'm looking for ways to use realistic artifacts and measure actual real world impacts.
  2. Most results rest on 20 edge cases per model per test (60 for the accessibility comparison). Due to budget constraints, I chose to limit my choice of variables and outputs tested to balance cost and reasonable confidence intervals.
  3. Directly comparing text and vision requires rendering words to an image, which introduces confounders beyond font. The rendering pipeline is identical across fonts, so font-to-font comparisons are apples-to-apples. Because the effect appears in every test, and its magnitude differs by model, I think this is a per-model characteristic.

Next steps

  1. Incorporate any feedback on methodology and gaps.
  2. Accessibility fonts. More assistive formats (large print, high contrast, letter spacing), more items, enough statistical power to pin down per-provider effects.
  3. Identify fonts that are unusually persuasive or more reliably flip decisions
  4. Run and publish the harness against major model releases.

Why build this?

I got into typography this year after reading “Thinking with Type.” Type design mirrors the tech and culture of the day. Since it’s 2026, I was curious about how agents are influenced by, and may potentially shape, popular fonts and design conventions. Is Comic Sans still seen as unprofessional? Would an agent then be less likely to recommend a resume written in OpenDyslexic? What does the future of typography look like when media’s increasingly consumed by both people and robots? ThinkingType is a side project to understand this a bit better from my day job as a product manager working in large-scale recommendations (and with a limited token budget).
