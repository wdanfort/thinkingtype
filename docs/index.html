ThinkingType: how typography influences judgments and decisions

Does a VLM “read” a Comic Sans resume as unprofessional?

Views are my own and not my employer’s · v1, last updated July 20th, 2026.

I built ThinkingType as a new, lightweight benchmark measuring how typography influences qualitative assessments (e.g. trustworthy, professional) and downstream decisions (“flag this policy violation,” “approve this resume”). v1 asks the same binary questions two ways — as a rendered image and as plain text to the same model — across 120 synthetic sentences and 330 synthetic artifacts. My main findings:

  1. Reading text as an image changes answers. Depending on model, 8 to 15% of yes/no judgments flip when the same model answers from an image instead of plain text.
  2. This vision-text divergence exists (and differs) across the 3 providers tested and changes with each new generation.
  3. Fonts influence decisions and judgments for VLMs at the margins.
    1. Times regular is the most stable font in every model I tested; plain serif is the closest thing to “reads like text.”
    2. Comic Neue (an open-source Comic Sans analogue, labeled “Comic Sans” in charts and tables below) and OpenDyslexic are the likeliest to flip a judgment, with as much as 21% of judgments flipping (OpenDyslexic on GPT-4o) and Comic Neue at 14% on both GPT-5.5 and GPT-5.6 Sol, roughly double their plain-serif baselines.
    3. In the accessibility comparison, OpenDyslexic skewed consistently harsher at the decision boundary: 17 of the 21 items that moved, moved toward removal. (This is a separate, larger test than the pooled decision shifts in the appendix.)
  4. Refusals are presentation-sensitive. Fable 5 refused six times more often on images than on identical text, and the refusals clustered by font.

I haven’t found prior work focused on downstream decisions and qualitative assessments. The recent work I’ve found on AI × typography focuses more on VLM accuracy, cybersecurity, and things like DecoyFont.

Given that these findings shift across providers and models, I’m planning to run this benchmark against future major model releases. All of the code, methodology, and data are publicly available on GitHub. Design and coding were done with Claude Code. I want feedback on methodology and gaps — especially from anyone running VLMs on real documents in production — so please reach out.

Quick Product Implications

  1. Vision-text consistency is not stable across provider, model, or generation. Typography should be included in internal product evals to identify drift — Reading, Not Thinking (2026) found font choice alone can swing accuracy by up to 47 points on reasoning tasks.
  2. Accessible fonts like OpenDyslexic fared much worse than traditional ones, so extra caution should be taken when building out evals to ensure fairness and equity. Relatedly, fonts like Times had the least text-vision divergence, so people might try to game AI systems by selecting “better” fonts — Visual Persuasion (2026) shows optimized image styling already sways VLM choices.
  3. Decision drift can be controlled. Clear prompt rubrics eliminated divergence for one of the three providers tested and cut it in half for a second.
  4. Measure refusals per format. Refusal rates differ between image and text presentation of the same content in Fable 5 (finding 4), so a single pooled refusal number will hide it.

Key Limitations

  1. All benchmark artifacts, including sentence examples, are synthetic, and so may not mirror real world results. I tried to generate them to be on the borderline for decisions and criteria. However, the effect appearing in every provider tested suggests it is not an artifact of a single model family — though real-world magnitudes remain untested.
  2. Most results rest on 20 edge cases per model per test (60 for the accessibility comparison). Due to budget constraints, I chose to limit my choice of variables and outputs tested to balance cost and reasonable confidence intervals.
  3. Stimuli are synthetic single-page documents with no names or demographics, by design. Real documents have messier content, but would make isolating to font alone more challenging.
  4. Directly comparing text and vision requires rendering words to an image, which introduces confounders beyond font. The rendering pipeline is identical across fonts, so font-to-font comparisons are apples-to-apples; the absolute image-vs-text gap should be read directionally.

Next steps

  1. Incorporate any feedback on methodology and gaps.
  2. Go deeper on accessibility. More assistive formats (large print, high contrast, letter spacing), more items, enough statistical power to pin down per-provider effects.
  3. Keep tracking releases. Continue running and publishing the eval harness against major model releases.

Why build this?

I got into typography this year after reading “Thinking with Type.” Type design mirrors the tech and culture of the day, so since it’s 2026, I was curious about how agents are influenced by, and may potentially shape, popular fonts and design conventions. Is Comic Sans still seen as unprofessional? Would an agent then be less likely to recommend a resume written in OpenDyslexic? What does the future of typography look like when it’s increasingly consumed by both people and robots? This project is a first-pass attempt to understand this.

typo-eval --config configs/gates_v1.yaml gates-analyze
