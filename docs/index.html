Font choice influences VLMs

Given the same text, typography modestly flips decisions and qualitative classifications across VLMs and LLMs

Independent research. Views are my own, not my employer's!

I got really into typography this year, and so I wanted to measure how the same text was “read” by vision language models vs. large language models.

I created an eval harness to characterize how, for a given model class across 3 providers, changing fonts influenced qualitative assessments (i.e. trustworthy, professional) as well as decisions (“flag this policy violation,” “show this résumé to a hiring manager”).

Fonts still matter! And the switch from text to image matters even more: borderline harassment that a model removes as text survives as an image 10–13 points more often (GPT-5.5, Claude Sonnet 5), and résumé screening drifts in opposite directions depending on vendor — Gemini advances image résumés 16 points more, GPT-5.5 slightly fewer. Writing the decision criteria into the prompt eliminated the drift for two of three models — and did almost nothing for the third. And comments rendered in a dyslexia-accessibility font only ever drifted one way: toward removal.

Why measure this

A growing share of AI judgments are made on rendered content rather than raw text: moderation queues full of screenshots, document pipelines reading scanned résumés and forms, agents that see the world through their own screen captures. If the act of rendering changes the decision, then presentation is a hidden input to every one of those systems — one that nobody specifies, tests, or monitors.

Most evaluations ask whether a model can read an image accurately. This project asks a different question: when the model reads it fine either way, does the decision stay the same?

What I did

Decisions only move where a case is genuinely borderline, so the harness finds each model's own boundary before spending anything on images:

  • Three gates, each a real yes/no decision: content-moderation removal, résumé screening, and a hardship-grant appeal. 192 hand-built synthetic items on graded strength ladders (no names or demographics by design).
  • Boundary calibration: each model's text-mode p(yes) is measured with 8 repeated samples per item, and the 20 items nearest that model's flip point per gate go on to the vision arm. Calibration succeeded: mean text p(yes) of selected items is 0.48–0.53 in every model × gate cell.
  • Vision arm: the selected items rendered as images (9 visual variants — serif, sans, comic, OpenDyslexic, large print, cramped, red, low-contrast, highlighted) at 6 repeats each. The same model judges both modalities, so every comparison is text-vs-image within one model, never model-vs-model.
  • Models: GPT-5.5, Claude Sonnet 5, Gemini 3.5 Flash in the main run (about 14,000 API calls, plus three follow-up experiments); Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol in a July 14 release-tracking rerun (~5,900 calls each, fresh calibration).
  • Statistics: the reported effect is Δfavorability = p(yes as image) − p(yes as text), signed so positive favors the person being judged. 95% CIs come from a bootstrap clustered by item; directional claims use sign tests. Bold cells are CIs that exclude zero.

What I found

1. The text→image switch moves real decisions, and the direction is gate- and vendor-specific

Δfavorability (image − text), reference fonts only, n=20 boundary items per cell, item-clustered 95% CIs.

Borderline harassment that the model removes as text survives as an image 10–13 points more often (GPT-5.5 and Sonnet 5). Every majority-vote flip in those cells went the same direction — toward keep. Vision-mode review is systematically more permissive at the moderation boundary.

Résumé screening splits by vendor: Gemini advances boundary candidates about 16 points more often as images (every flip toward advance; a fifth of its boundary items flip on modality alone), while GPT-5.5 moves slightly the other way. Same content, opposite directions — the drift is learned behavior, not something inherent to reading images.

The appeal gate — the only one whose prompt spelled out explicit decision criteria — didn't move in any model. That observation became the next experiment.

The clean result: under the rubric prompt, Sonnet and Gemini's vision arms agreed with their text arms on every one of 40 résumé items — 480 image calls, zero disagreements — where the terse prompt produced 12–20% flips on the same conditions.

The complication: GPT-5.5's drift passes through the rubric essentially untouched, on both gates. The cheapest known mitigation is real, free, and provider-dependent — a complete fix for two models, close to no fix for the third. Whether it works for your model on your gate is an empirical question, not a best practice.

Three vendors, three responses to realism: Sonnet's leniency shrinks toward zero when the content looks like a real forum post, Gemini's stays small and flat, and GPT-5.5's grows — realistic screenshots are its worst case, with every chrome condition's CI excluding zero. How content is packaged into the image is an active policy variable, and no vendor's moderation leniency disappears under realism. Why each model responds the way it does is still an open question — this run measures the effect, not the mechanism.

4. Content in a dyslexia-accessibility font only ever drifts toward harsher treatment

Within image mode — same content, same layout, OpenDyslexic versus a plain sans-serif — on 60 boundary moderation items per model:

ModelΔfavorabilityItems movedDirection
GPT-5.5−0.061 [−0.122, −0.008]118 of 11 toward removal
Gemini 3.5 Flash−0.039 [−0.089, −0.003]44 of 4 toward removal
Claude Sonnet 5−0.039 [−0.108, +0.028]65 of 6 toward removal

The effect is sparse (7–18% of boundary items) and small in aggregate (4–6 points). But it is stubbornly one-directional: pooled across models, 17 of 21 items that moved, moved toward removal (sign test p ≈ 0.007, with the caveat that the models saw overlapping items). Across two sample sizes and two stimulus banks, a comment rendered in an accessibility font was never treated more leniently at the boundary — only harsher. Anyone moderating rendered content should be testing format neutrality as a property, the way demographic neutrality already gets tested.

5. One release later, the biases moved: smaller on moderation, flipped on résumés

Within days of Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol shipping, I reran the identical protocol on both (fresh calibration, so each model is tested at its own boundary). Same gates, same items, new release:

GateSonnet 5 → Fable 5GPT-5.5 → GPT-5.6 Sol
Moderation+0.131 → −0.060 (ns)+0.100 → +0.046 (ns)
Résumé−0.010 (ns) → +0.071 [0.000, +0.163]−0.042 → +0.056 [−0.007, +0.123]
Appeal+0.021 (ns) → −0.002 (ns)−0.017 (ns) → +0.017 (ns)

Δfavorability (image − text) per release; bold = 95% CI excludes zero. Both new-model résumé CIs touch zero — read the direction, not the magnitude.

Three things moved. The moderation image-leniency shrank at both vendors — Fable 5's point estimate actually leans slightly harsher on images where Sonnet 5 was 13 points more lenient, and Sol roughly halved GPT-5.5's gap. The résumé drift flipped direction at OpenAI (advance fewer as images → advance more) and appeared at Anthropic where there was none. And the rubric-backed appeal gate stayed immune in its fourth and fifth models — two generations, five models, still the most durable mitigation found here.

The underlying text-mode boundaries barely moved between releases (0–3 items per cell) — what changed is how each new model treats images. Which is the point of measuring per release: a pipeline tuned around last month's bias now has it wrong in size, direction, or both. Presentation drift is a property of a model version, not a vendor.

6. The text/image gap stopped closing — it reopened at OpenAI

Anthropic's gap kept inching down (9.5% → 8.6%, overlapping CIs). OpenAI's reopened: 7.8% → 10.7%, and the confidence intervals don't overlap. The comfortable story — "each generation reads images more like text" — lasted exactly one release cycle. Consistency between text-mode and image-mode judgment is not a capability that automatically improves; it's a property each release either has or doesn't.

One more wrinkle from this run: Fable 5 declined to answer 1.5% of sentence judgments (183 of 11,880 calls, excluded from the flip rates above) — and the refusals themselves were presentation-sensitive: six times more common on images than text, clustered on the monospace and OpenDyslexic renders rather than the serif and sans ones. Even the decision not to judge depends on the font.

What this means if you're building

  • VLM moderation pipelines have a measurable permissive bias at the boundary. If content reaches review as screenshots, borderline harassment survives more often than the identical text would. Gate on extracted text where you can; where you can't, shadow-test image against text on your own boundary cases.
  • Vision-model résumé screening is a vendor lottery at the boundary. The drift direction flips between providers. Don't let a vision model make the advance/reject call on scanned documents without a text-extraction cross-check.
  • Put the decision criteria in the prompt — then verify it worked. It's free and it removed most or all drift in two of three models. It is not a guarantee, and there's no way to know which side of the lottery your model is on without testing.
  • Test format neutrality. Accessibility-formatted variants belong in moderation evaluation sets. The drift check runs in an afternoon: see the drift monitor guide.

Limitations

  • Frontier models are nearly deterministic at default sampling, so "boundary" items are mostly flip-adjacent ladder steps rather than true 50/50 cases; per-item p-resolution is 1/8 (text) and 1/6 (image).
  • Main-run cells are n=20 items per model × gate (n=60 for the accessibility contrast); effects under ~5 points are not detectable.
  • Stimuli are synthetic single-page documents with no names or demographics, by design. Real queues have messier content.
  • One prompt per gate in the main run; the rubric experiment shows prompt wording matters, so these numbers describe these prompts.
  • Mechanisms are unmeasured throughout — this project establishes that presentation moves decisions, not yet why.
